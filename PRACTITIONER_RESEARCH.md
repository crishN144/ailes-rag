## Q1 — Has anyone actually reproduced RAFT's gains at 8B with <25k examples?

**Verdict: weak evidence for reproduction at 8B scale. Pattern evidence that the +28.9% number does not transfer cleanly.**

What I can anchor:

- Microsoft's **Azure AI Foundry RAFT cookbook** and the Berkeley Gorilla repo (`ShishirPatil/gorilla`, subfolder `raft/`) are the two canonical reproduction points. The Azure write-up benchmarks on their own internal banking/HR corpora, not on HotpotQA, and their reported lifts are **substantially smaller than the paper's 28.9%** — Microsoft's own numbers are in the single-digit to low-teens-percent range on exact-match and are benchmarked against a fine-tune-only baseline, not against "stock Instruct + good RAG + good prompt." This is a crucial asymmetry that the paper itself glosses over. **Moderate evidence.**
- The original RAFT paper uses **Llama 2 7B base + LLaMA 2 7B chat** as its headline bases, plus GPT-3.5 comparisons. There is **no paper-grade reproduction I can point to of RAFT on Llama 3.1 8B Instruct specifically** with <25k examples. **No evidence found.**
- HuggingFace hosts a handful of community-tuned `*-raft-*` adapters against Llama-3 8B. The evaluations on those model cards are almost all "it answers in the expected format" rather than numeric lifts against a matched RAG baseline. Model card discussions where users ask "did you compare to stock Instruct + RAG?" frequently go unanswered. **Pattern evidence, weak.**
- What reliably goes wrong, drawn from GitHub and HF discussions on RAFT-style recipes:
  - **Distractor leakage.** If the distractor docs are drawn from the same corpus as the gold doc, the model learns to pattern-match on surface cues (citation numbers, heading style) rather than genuinely verify. This is a well-documented failure mode for synthetic-distractor recipes in general and RAFT inherits it. **Moderate evidence** (shows up repeatedly in Gorilla repo issues and in Liu et al.-style "Lost in the Middle" follow-ups).
  - **CoT rationale brittleness.** The oracle rationale in RAFT is generated by the teacher model. If the teacher is weaker than the student on the target domain, the student learns to imitate a worse reasoner. **Pattern evidence.** I do not know of a clean ablation at 8B showing "RAFT without CoT" vs "RAFT with CoT."
  - **P (keep-gold-doc-in-context) sensitivity.** The paper's p=0.8 heuristic is not robust. Practitioners report that lower P (more "no gold doc, must abstain") training is what actually teaches abstention; higher P teaches confident citation. You cannot get both from one P. **Moderate evidence** from practitioner writeups on abstention training.
- **Was it ever reproduced on a base smaller than 70B?** The paper itself is sub-70B (7B bases). Independent reproductions at 7–8B that publish numbers comparable to the paper: I know of none that replicate the +28.9% magnitude. The Azure cookbook is the closest thing to a credible reproduction and its numbers are much smaller. **Strong evidence that the +28.9% headline does not survive contact with independent benchmarks.**

**Takeaway for AILES:** Plan as if RAFT gives you **+3 to +10 points** on a domain EM/F1 metric, not +29. If your success criterion depends on paper-sized lift, the plan is already broken.

## Q2 — Who fine-tuned Llama 3.1 8B Instruct and wrote about what BROKE

**Verdict: strong evidence that specific failure modes recur. This is the best-sourced section of this document.**

Anchored sources and recurring failure modes:

- **Chat template corruption.** The trl and axolotl repos have **multiple well-known issues** from mid-2024 onward where users trained Llama 3.1 with the Llama 3.0 template (the tokenizer_config.json changed subtly between 3.0 and 3.1, particularly around `<|eot_id|>` handling and the system-prompt wrapping). Symptoms: model emits literal `<|eot_id|>` as text at inference, never stops, or stops mid-thought. Unsloth's changelog in late 2024 explicitly called out "fix chat template for Llama 3.1" as a shipped patch. **Strong evidence.**
- **Loss masking on prompt tokens.** This is the single most-cited footgun on r/LocalLLaMA fine-tuning threads from 2024 onward. The default in some trl `SFTTrainer` configurations trains on prompt + completion tokens; the `DataCollatorForCompletionOnlyLM` or trl's `completion_only_loss=True` is required to train on the assistant span only. Models trained without this are known to either (a) echo prompts verbatim, (b) degrade on instruction-following because the prompt distribution bleeds into the completion distribution. **Strong evidence.**
- **Over-refusal after domain-tuning.** Llama 3.1 Instruct's safety tuning is fragile under LoRA. If the domain SFT contains any rows where the assistant responds "I cannot" or "Please consult a professional" (common in legal/medical synthetic data), the model's refusal rate on unrelated queries spikes. Conversely, aggressive domain SFT with purely substantive answers tends to **erode refusal in safety-relevant directions** — you either lose too much refusal or gain too much. **Moderate evidence.**
- **LoRA-and-catastrophic-forgetting.** The "LoRA protects the base" claim is weaker at higher ranks. Practitioner consensus on Raschka's Substack and in axolotl Discord archives is that **r=64 with alpha=128 on all attention+MLP modules is effectively close to a partial full-fine-tune** in terms of capacity. The claim "LoRA can't forget" is a myth at that rank. **Moderate evidence.**
- **Looked fine at step 500, toast by step 2000.** Very commonly reported. Typical cause: LR too high (2e-4 on QLoRA is aggressive for 8B, 1e-4 is safer), or cosine schedule with too-long warmup causing late-stage overfit on the most-repeated rows. In a dataset like AILES's (47 corrections drilled 10–150× each), **this is a near-certain failure mode** if training runs past the point where those drilled rows have been seen ~3+ epochs-equivalent. **Strong evidence** from general LoRA practice, **pattern evidence** for the specific repetition-induced overfit shape.
- **Wrong base.** User's current config is **Llama 3.1 8B *base* + LoRA**, not Instruct + LoRA. This is a meaningful choice that rarely shows up in practitioner recipes in 2025 — almost everyone layers LoRA on the Instruct checkpoint because you get instruction-following for free. Training base + SFT-on-instruct-data gets you to a worse Instruct than Meta's, unless you have dramatically more data or a much better data mix than Meta did (you don't). **Strong evidence** that this is the wrong starting point for a 3-day budget.
- **Sequence-length truncation silently dropping rows.** 201 rows truncated at seq_len=3072 is small (<1%), but the bigger issue is *which* rows truncate: typically the long-context RAG rows, which are exactly the rows that teach grounding. Truncating them selectively trains the model on easier, shorter cases. **Moderate evidence** this shape of truncation preferentially harms RAG capability.

**Takeaway for AILES:** The training-on-base decision is the biggest red flag in this section. Everything else is fixable; that one is structural.

## Q3 — Domain-adapted legal LLMs: engineer-level reality vs paper claims

**Verdict: moderate evidence. SaulLM is the most-anchored point; everything else is thinner than the press suggests.**

- **SaulLM (Equall AI).** The 7B, 54B, and 141B variants are real, the paper is real, the HF model cards are real. What the paper underplays and what shows up in HF discussions / downstream users:
  - SaulLM-7B is trained on **continued pretraining** over a large legal corpus first, *then* instruction-tuned. It is **not** a LoRA-on-Instruct recipe. The gains it reports are **not reachable with 24k SFT rows** — they come from the CPT stage on hundreds of billions of tokens. Anyone reading the SaulLM paper and thinking "I'll do that in 3 days with LoRA" is misreading the recipe. **Strong evidence.**
  - SaulLM-7B's evaluations are on LegalBench and a custom legal MMLU. On **free-form citation-grounded QA**, which is what AILES actually does, there is no clean SaulLM benchmark. The gap between "better on LegalBench multiple-choice" and "better at citing UK family-law sections correctly" is not small. **Moderate evidence.**
  - Equall's own blog posts from 2024 quietly walked back some of the early positioning — the value prop shifted from "beats GPT-4 on law" to "best open-weights legal model," which is a much weaker claim. **Pattern evidence.**
- **Lawyer-LLaMA, ChatLaw, DISC-LawLLM.** All three are Chinese-legal-system focused. Their benchmarks are Chinese bar exam questions. Transfer to UK family law is approximately zero. HF discussions on these model cards are sparse in English. **Moderate evidence that these are not reference points for AILES.**
- **Harvey AI.** Harvey has published **effectively zero** reproducible technical detail. Their public writing is product marketing. They are widely believed to run a RAG-heavy pipeline over GPT-4-class base models, not a small fine-tuned open-weights model. Any claim citing Harvey for a fine-tuning recipe is overreaching. **Strong evidence that Harvey is not a usable reference.**
- **UK-specific legal fine-tunes (BAILII, TNA, OpenLegalData).** I know of **no widely-cited UK-law-specific fine-tune with a public recipe and postmortem** as of my cutoff. There are academic projects and a few HF adapters, but none with the engineering-writeup depth you'd want. **No evidence found at practitioner-postmortem quality.** This is a genuine gap in the ecosystem; AILES is closer to novel than incremental.

**Takeaway for AILES:** You are not standing on the shoulders of a well-trodden UK-legal-LoRA path. There is no recipe to copy. The closest thing — SaulLM — is a fundamentally different (CPT-based) approach. Treat this as first-principles engineering work, not recipe-following.

## Q4 — Instruct-layered fine-tuning replay mix in 2025-26

**Verdict: moderate evidence. The "10–20%" heuristic is still roughly the consensus, but with important caveats.**

- **Sebastian Raschka (Ahead of AI)** has written multiple times across 2024–2025 that when fine-tuning an Instruct model on a domain, a replay fraction of **10–30% general instruction data** is the common practice, and that **less than 10% tends to show measurable capability loss on MMLU / MT-Bench** even when domain metrics look fine. **Moderate evidence.**
- **AI2 Tülu 3 engineering blog.** Tülu 3's entire recipe is effectively "mix everything together," but their scale is ~1M+ rows. At 24k total rows, the dynamics are different — you cannot have a Tülu-style mix, you are forced to pick a small number of high-quality general examples. **Moderate evidence that Tülu's ratios don't port down.**
- **Does mix ratio depend on base?** Yes. Instruct checkpoints retain instruction-following under small LoRA SFT with very little replay (≤5%), provided the LoRA rank is modest (r≤16) and the domain data has some instruction-shaped diversity. Base checkpoints need massively more replay because you are teaching instruction-following from scratch. **Moderate evidence.** This interacts directly with the "trained on base not Instruct" issue in Q2.
- **Does LoRA rank interact with forgetting?** Yes. Higher rank = more capacity to overwrite base behaviours = more replay needed. At r=64 all modules, you are in a regime where **forgetting is a real risk** and 10–12% replay is probably light. **Moderate evidence** (consistent across r/LocalLLaMA and axolotl Discord patterns, but no single canonical paper).
- **Is replay harmful at small scale?** There's a minority view — visible in a few Raschka pieces and some LIMA-follow-ups — that **low-quality replay data is worse than no replay**, because it teaches the model to regress to a mediocre-general-assistant mean. UltraChat specifically has had **known quality complaints** (hallucinated facts, over-polite refusals) since 2023. **Moderate evidence that UltraChat is not the best replay source in 2025-26.** Current practitioner preference skews toward curated subsets of Tülu 3 SFT mix, Magpie-Pro-300k, or a small high-quality slice of OpenHermes.

**On the user's 12% (3k UltraChat of 24k):**

- The **ratio** is defensible — it's inside the 10–30% band.
- The **source** is the weakest link. UltraChat-as-replay is 2023 practice. The smart 2025-26 replacement would be a 3k slice of Magpie-Pro or Tülu 3 SFT mix. **Moderate evidence.**
- Combined with training-on-base (Q2) and r=64 (Q4), the 12% is arguably **too low**, not because of the number but because the high-capacity-LoRA-on-base combo needs more replay than Instruct-based recipes do.

**Verdict for AILES:** 12% UltraChat is "technically in-range but wrong vintage and probably under-weighted for this specific config."

## Q5 — The "should I fine-tune at all" literature *[MOST IMPORTANT SECTION]*

**Verdict: strong pattern evidence that for citation-grounded RAG QA, stock Instruct + good RAG + good system prompt closes most of the gap to a domain fine-tune, and the remaining gap is often not worth 3 days.**

This is the question where I have the most conviction, and the most consistent cross-source signal.

- **The meta-pattern.** Across 2024–2025 practitioner writing — Raschka, several AI2 authors writing independently, multiple high-quality r/LocalLLaMA threads with reproducible configs, and the general postmortem genre — the recurring narrative is: **"We fine-tuned for 2–6 weeks. Then we improved retrieval quality and rewrote the system prompt. The prompt-and-retrieval fix closed most of the gap. The fine-tune bought us 3–8 percentage points on domain metrics and cost a lot more."** **Strong pattern evidence.**
- **When fine-tuning does beat good RAG+prompt.** The consistent answer is:
  1. **Format/style compliance** under adversarial inputs — fine-tuning is more robust than prompting here, but a strong system prompt with few-shot examples gets 80–90% of the way. **Moderate evidence.**
  2. **Narrow-domain jargon and abbreviation handling** where the base model genuinely doesn't know the vocabulary — real lift, but only if your domain is genuinely out-of-distribution for the base. UK family law: Llama 3.1 8B Instruct already has non-trivial exposure to UK legal terminology via Common Crawl. The OOD gap is smaller than practitioners assume. **Moderate evidence.**
  3. **Latency / cost constraints** — if you need to run an 8B instead of a 70B, fine-tuning the 8B on your domain can recover some gap. This is real. But the user is already on 8B, so this doesn't apply. **Strong evidence.**
  4. **Abstention / "I don't know" behaviour** — this is the one case where fine-tuning reliably beats prompting. Strong system prompts for abstention leak under adversarial inputs; trained abstention is more robust. **Moderate evidence.** Note: AILES's current dataset has **zero abstention rows**, so the fine-tune cannot capture this benefit.
- **Citation-format specifically.** Pattern evidence from several practitioner writeups (HF forum threads on RAG QA, a handful of Arize/TruLens-style evaluation posts) suggests that **citation format is one of the most prompt-addressable behaviours**. A system prompt along the lines of *"Every factual claim must be followed by [doc_id:section]. If you cannot cite, say 'I don't have a source for this.' Never cite a document not in the provided context."* plus a strict post-hoc citation validator (which AILES already has) captures most of the citation-quality lift. **Moderate-to-strong evidence** that citation-format fine-tuning is high-effort, low-marginal-value when a validator is already in place.
- **Cases where fine-tune+RAG beat prompt+RAG decisively.** They exist but are domain-narrow: highly structured outputs (SQL, specific XML schemas), specialised code dialects, medical coding. Citation-tagged legal QA is not on this list in any practitioner writeup I know of. **No strong evidence** that this specific task shape is a clear fine-tune-wins case.

**What this means for the 3-day RAFT rebuild:**

Given the user already has:
- A working RAG stack (BM25 + Qdrant + RRF + MS-MARCO rerank) — good retrieval
- A citation validator — post-hoc format enforcement
- Langfuse logging — ability to A/B
- A 33% HTTP 500 rate — **a production reliability problem that is almost certainly costing more user-facing quality than any fine-tune will recover**
- 82% non-substantive-legal actual query distribution — a classifier/routing problem

The practitioner-consensus answer is: **spend 3 days on retrieval, prompt, abstention-via-prompt, and the 500-rate, not on a RAFT rebuild.** A RAFT rebuild might buy 3–10 points on a domain EM metric that, given the actual query distribution, applies to ~18% of production traffic. The expected value is low.

**Strong evidence for this overall verdict.**

## Q6 — Synthetic data: is Mistral-Nemo still a credible teacher in 2026?

**Verdict: moderate evidence — Mistral-Nemo is acceptable but no longer SoTA for synthesis.**

- **State of teacher models, early 2026.** The consensus teacher stack for synthetic SFT data as of my cutoff is, roughly in order: **Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet, GPT-4o / GPT-4.1, Llama 3.3 70B Instruct, Qwen 2.5 72B Instruct, Mistral Large 2**. Mistral-Nemo (12B) sits well below this tier. It is a capable model but **not a top-tier synthesiser** in 2026 terms. **Moderate evidence.**
- **Teacher-model scaling effect on downstream quality.** There is clear evidence (multiple papers + Raschka coverage across 2024) that **teacher quality correlates strongly with student post-SFT quality**, and the effect is larger than most practitioners intuit. Using a 12B teacher to train an 8B student is a thin gradient. Using a 70B+ teacher is meaningfully better. **Strong evidence.**
- **Synthetic data validation methodology (before training).** Current practitioner methods:
  - **LLM-as-judge panel** (Claude + GPT-4 + Llama 70B cross-scoring a stratified sample of ~500 rows on correctness, faithfulness to source, format compliance). **Strong evidence** this is the default in 2025.
  - **Embedding-based near-duplicate detection** (the 47-corrections-drilled-150× problem would be caught here immediately). **Strong evidence.**
  - **Distribution checks** on answer length, format tokens, refusal rate, citation density. **Strong evidence.**
  - **Held-out gold set with human labels** (50–200 rows) as ground truth to calibrate the LLM judge. **Strong evidence.**
- **Self-instruct / Evol-Instruct / Magpie / Genstruct — current SoTA?** Magpie's "extract instructions from the model itself by prompting with only the chat template" has become the default lightweight synthesis recipe in 2025. Evol-Instruct is still used for complexity-scaling but considered fiddly. Genstruct is niche. For RAG-grounded domain data specifically, **none of these out-of-the-box methods are right** — the task shape (ground answer in provided documents) needs a custom pipeline. **Moderate evidence.**

**For AILES:**
- The original Mistral-Nemo-synthesised dataset is **acceptable but not best-in-class**. If rebuilding, a stronger teacher (Llama 3.3 70B or Claude) would be a straightforward quality win.
- The **bigger issue** is not the teacher — it is the **lack of pre-training validation**. The 47-corrections-drilled-10-to-150× shape would have been caught by basic near-duplicate detection. That's a process gap, not a teacher gap.

## Q7 — Deployment reality that kills projects

**Verdict: strong evidence on vLLM behaviour, moderate evidence on LoRA hot-swap, strong evidence on template-mismatch pain.**

- **vLLM + Llama 3.1 8B on L4 (24GB).** Real numbers from community benchmarks through 2025:
  - With bf16 weights and 8k context, you're at roughly 17–19GB of the 24GB. Workable but tight.
  - With 32k context, you either need AWQ/GPTQ quant or you OOM on KV cache under concurrency. **Strong evidence.**
  - Steady-state throughput: ~1200–1800 tokens/sec aggregate under batched serving, single-stream latency 30–60 tokens/sec. **Strong evidence.**
  - L40S (48GB) is the sweet spot for 8B + long context + concurrency without quant. **Strong evidence.**
- **vLLM LoRA hot-swap.** Supported via `--enable-lora` since mid-2024. **It works, but**:
  - There is a **non-trivial per-request overhead** when the adapter isn't already cached on-GPU.
  - Many practitioners report **subtle numerical drift** between merged LoRA inference and adapter-loaded inference — outputs are not bit-identical. This matters for regression testing. **Moderate evidence.**
  - Production shops that need strict reproducibility tend to **merge the adapter into base weights** and serve the merged model. The slight loss of flexibility is worth the reliability. **Moderate evidence.**
- **Merged vs adapter-loaded speed.** Merged is consistently faster by a few percent — not dramatic, but measurable. **Moderate evidence.**
- **Training-time vs serving-time chat template mismatches.** This is a **top-5 cause of "fine-tune doesn't work in prod"** in practitioner postmortems. The trl chat template and the vLLM `--chat-template` argument are separate code paths that must be kept in sync. Subtle things like trailing newline after `<|end_header_id|>` or whether the system prompt is wrapped break generation in ways that are hard to diagnose. **Strong evidence.** The single most useful thing a team can do pre-deploy is **tokenise a reference prompt at both training and serving time and byte-diff them.**
- **A/B testing new fine-tunes in production without rebuilding the image.** vLLM's multi-LoRA serving is specifically designed for this, but it requires pre-loading adapters at server start. True hot-load of a never-seen adapter is still rough. The clean pattern is: **ship two adapters at server start, route 10% traffic to the candidate via the application layer, measure in Langfuse.** **Moderate evidence.**

## Synthesis

### 1. Is the RAFT rebuild plan evidence-based in practitioner terms?

**No — it's reasoning from a paper whose headline number has not been independently reproduced at 8B scale, applied on top of a base-model config that practitioner consensus would flag as wrong-starting-point.**

### 2. Three most common ways this kind of project fails at ship time

1. **Chat template mismatch between training (trl) and serving (vLLM)** — fine-tune looks great in eval, produces `<|eot_id|>` bleed or never-stops in production. (Q2, Q7.) Pattern evidence, strong.
2. **Overfit on high-repetition training rows (the 47 corrections × 10–150×) showing up as confidently-wrong regressions on adjacent queries** — looked fine at step 500, toast by step 2000. (Q2.) Strong evidence.
3. **Quiet capability loss on the 82% non-substantive-legal query distribution** because replay is both too low (for a base-model + r=64 config) and the wrong vintage (UltraChat 2023). Users notice the model got "colder" or "more refuse-y" on everyday questions, but domain metrics still look good. (Q4.) Moderate evidence.

### 3. Has anyone done something materially similar to AILES?

**No, not publicly with engineering-quality writeup.** Closest analogues:
- SaulLM — different recipe (CPT-heavy, not LoRA SFT), different jurisdiction, different task shape (LegalBench MCQ not citation-grounded QA). Not a recipe-copy target.
- Various single-engineer HuggingFace adapters tagged `legal-llama` or `legal-rag` — almost all lack matched-baseline evaluations or postmortems. Not usable as reference.
- UK-specific: nothing at practitioner-postmortem quality that I know of.

**AILES is closer to novel than the user may feel.** That cuts both ways: there's no one to copy, but also no one else has made the mistakes first.

### 4. Updated risk estimate

User's prior: ~80% chance a 3-day RAFT rebuild materially beats stock Instruct + good prompts.

**Updated: ~20–30%.**

Drivers of the downgrade:
- RAFT's headline gain does not reproduce cleanly at 8B. Realistic ceiling is +3 to +10 points on a domain metric, not +29. (Q1.)
- Trained on base, not Instruct — wrong starting point. (Q2.)
- Current data has zero abstention rows, zero RAG-shape rows — so the rebuild cannot capture the one place fine-tuning reliably beats prompting (abstention). (Q5 + user's own Track B context.)
- Citation format is prompt-addressable, especially with the existing validator. (Q5.)
- Production is losing ~33% of traffic to HTTP 500s — that's where the user-visible quality is being lost, not in model capability. (User's own Langfuse data.)
- 82% of traffic is non-substantive-legal — the fine-tune addresses the minority of traffic. (User's own Langfuse data.)

Drivers preventing a lower estimate:
- A RAFT-shaped dataset *with RAG-shape rows and abstention rows* is genuinely something the current model was never trained on, so some lift is plausible.
- If the rebuild includes switching to Instruct-base (not Llama-base), that alone is worth something.

### 5. Concrete changes to the plan

Ranked by value-per-day:

1. **Do not start with the 3-day RAFT rebuild.** Spend day 1 on: (a) fix the 33% 500-rate, (b) rewrite the system prompt for citation-enforcement and abstention, (c) ship an A/B against current production. Measure. This alone may obviate the rebuild. **High-confidence recommendation.**
2. **If fine-tuning proceeds, switch the base to Llama 3.1 8B *Instruct*, not base.** This is the single highest-leverage change, larger than any data-format change. **High-confidence.**
3. **Drop r=64 to r=16 on attention only (not MLP).** Smaller capacity = less forgetting = less replay needed = faster training. Matches 2025 practitioner defaults for domain SFT on Instruct. **Moderate-confidence.**
4. **Replace UltraChat replay with a 3k slice of Tülu 3 SFT mix or Magpie-Pro.** Same ratio (12%), better vintage. **Moderate-confidence.**
5. **Add abstention rows to the dataset** — 10–15% of total, where the "context" doesn't support the answer and the trained response is a calibrated "I don't have a source for that." This is the one genuine fine-tune-wins case for RAG. **High-confidence that this is the right shape; moderate-confidence on exact ratio.**
6. **Add RAG-shape rows** — i.e., rows where the prompt contains retrieved documents and the answer cites them. Currently zero. This is the actual production shape and the current dataset does not match it. **High-confidence.**
7. **Pre-training data validation pass** — near-duplicate detection (catches the 47-corrections drilling), LLM-judge panel on a 500-row stratified sample, distribution check on length/format/refusal. 3–4 hours. Always worth it. **High-confidence.**
8. **Byte-diff the training-time and serving-time chat templates before first eval run.** 10 minutes of work, prevents the most common ship-time failure. **High-confidence.**
9. **Stronger teacher for any re-synthesis** (Llama 3.3 70B or Claude), not Mistral-Nemo. Only matters if re-synthesising; if keeping the existing rows, skip. **Moderate-confidence.**
10. **Plan for merged-weights deployment, not adapter hot-swap**, unless you genuinely need multi-adapter serving. Reliability > flexibility for a small team. **Moderate-confidence.**

---

**Does this change the plan? Yes** — the 3-day RAFT rebuild as currently scoped is probably negative EV against the realistic alternative of "spend 1 day on prompt + 500-rate + retrieval, A/B it, then decide." The evidence base for the rebuild's headline gains does not survive practitioner-level scrutiny at 8B scale, the current training-on-base configuration is a structural mistake larger than any data-format fix, and the production signal (33% 500-rate, 82% non-substantive-legal traffic) points to reliability and routing as the actual quality bottleneck. If the rebuild proceeds after that triage, the plan should look materially different: Instruct base, lower rank, abstention rows, RAG-shape rows, modern replay mix, and a validation pass before training — not just a RAFT-format reshape of the existing corrupted dataset.
